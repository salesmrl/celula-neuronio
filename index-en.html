What are Neurons?

Neurons are the basic units of the nervous system and generate electrical signals that transmit information rapidly over long distances.

Neuron Structure

A neuron is composed of several specialized parts
that work together to allow communication between nerve cells.
CELL BODY

Also known as soma, it is the control center
of the neuron. Here, the genetic information
is stored in the nucleus and protein synthesis
and cell maintenance occur.

DENDRITES

Dendrites are extensions branching from the
cell body and receive signals from other
neurons or sensory cells. They have receptors
that detect neurotransmitters released by other
neurons, allowing the signal to be transmitted.

AXON

The axon is a long, thin extension that
transmits signals to other neurons or
effector cells. It is covered by a myelin sheath,
which helps speed up signal transmission. The axon terminals are the ends of the axon that release neurotransmitters, chemical substances that transmit signals to other neurons or cells.

SYNAPSES

Synapses are junctions between two neurons or between
a neuron and an effector cell. They allow
communication between nerve cells, enabling signals to be transmitted efficiently and precisely.

MYELIN SHEATH

The myelin sheath surrounds the axon of some neurons, playing a crucial role in transmitting nerve signals.

Neuron Functions

Neurons have several important functions in the nervous system. They are responsible for transmitting electrical and chemical signals to other neurons or cells, processing sensory, motor, and cognitive information, and communicating with each other through synapses. In addition, neurons integrate signals from different sources to generate appropriate responses and control body functions such as movement, sensation, cognition, and emotion. There are different types of neuronal functions, including sensory, motor, and cognitive functions.
SENSORY FUNCTIONS involve detecting sensory stimuli and transmitting information to the central nervous system. Motor functions involve transmitting signals to muscles and glands, controlling movement and other body functions.
COGNITIVE FUNCTIONS are involved in processes such as thinking, perception, attention, and memory. In summary, neurons play a fundamental role in transmitting and processing information in the nervous system, allowing the body to respond to stimuli and perform complex functions.

Types of Neurons

    Neurons by Function:

  1. Sensory neurons: Detect sensory stimuli from the environment.
  2. Motor neurons: Transmit commands to muscles and glands.
  3. Association neurons: Integrate sensory and motor information.
  4. Cognitive neurons: Involved in processes such as thinking and memory.
  5. Emotional neurons: Regulate emotional responses.

    6. Neurons by Morphology:

  6. Multipolar neurons: Have many dendrites and one axon.
  7. Bipolar neurons: Have two extensions, one axon and one dendrite.
  8. Unipolar neurons: Have only one extension, the axon.
  9. Pyramidal neurons: Have a triangular shape.
  10. Purkinje cells: Have a tree-like shape.

    11. Neurons by Location:

  11. Cerebral cortex neurons: Involved in cognitive processes.
  12. Cerebellum neurons: Regulate movement and coordination.
  13. Spinal cord neurons: Transmit signals between the brain and the body.
  14. Brainstem neurons: Regulate autonomic functions.
  15. Peripheral nervous system neurons: Transmit signals between the central nervous system and the body.

    16. Neurons by Neurotransmitter:

  16. Dopaminergic neurons: Release dopamine, involved in reward and motivation.
  17. Serotonergic neurons: Release serotonin, involved in mood and appetite.
  18. GABAergic neurons: Release GABA, an inhibitory neurotransmitter.
  19. Glutamatergic neurons: Release glutamate, an excitatory neurotransmitter.
  20. Cholinergic neurons: Release acetylcholine, involved in memory and movement.

    21. Other Types of Neurons:

  21. Medium spiny neurons: Involved in movement regulation.
  22. Local circuit neurons: Connect nearby neurons.
  23. Projection neurons: Transmit signals to other parts of the brain.
  24. Interneurons: Connect other neurons.
  25. Fast response neurons: Respond quickly to stimuli.
  26. Slow response neurons: Respond slowly to stimuli.
  27. Tonic neurons: Maintain constant activity.
  28. Phasic neurons: Respond to changes in stimuli.
  29. Primary sensory neurons: Detect primary sensory stimuli.
  30. Secondary sensory neurons: Process secondary sensory information.
    31. Specific Neurons:
  31. Betz cells: Giant pyramidal neurons in the motor cortex.
  32. Renshaw cells: Inhibitory interneurons in the spinal cord.
  33. Rety neurons: Specific information not found.
  34. Martinotti neurons: Inhibitory interneurons in the cerebral cortex.
  35. Cajal-Retzius neurons: Important neurons in cortex development.

Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitters are chemical substances produced by neurons that play a key role in transmitting signals between nerve cells. They are released by the axon terminals of neurons and bind to specific receptors in other neurons or cells, allowing communication between nerve cells.

Neurotransmitter Functions:

Signal Transmission: neurotransmitters transmit signals between neurons, enabling communication.
Regulation of Body Functions: neurotransmitters regulate body functions such as movement, sensation, cognition, and emotion.
Response Modulation: neurotransmitters can modulate the responses of neurons and cells, influencing how the body reacts to stimuli.

Types of Neurotransmitters:

Excitatory Neurotransmitters: stimulate neuronal activity, increasing the likelihood of firing.
Inhibitory Neurotransmitters: reduce neuronal activity, decreasing the likelihood of firing.

Examples of Neurotransmitters:


Dopamine: involved in regulating movement, motivation, and reward.
Serotonin: involved in regulating mood, appetite, and sleep.
Acetylcholine: involved in regulating movement and memory.
